FRACTALS:

a shape that looks the same no matter how far you zoom in

What are fractals

Fractals are infinitely detailed shapes, usually built from a seemingly simple equation repeated over and over on its own output. The swirling pattern behind this page is a Julia set — it comes from one single number, endlessly added to itself and multiplied by itself.

Zoom into any edge of a fractal and you'll find the same kind of detail you started with, forever. No matter the magnification, the shape never resolves into something simple. That property is called self-similarity, and it's the signature of every fractal, from snowflakes to river deltas to the branching of your own lungs.

Fractals in the real world

Fractals are more than an abstract concept — they show up in everyday life. One major application is that we do not have an accurate measurement of any coastline. As the portion of coastline measured becomes smaller, it becomes more detailed, leading the coastline's measured length to grow toward infinity. This is known as the coastline paradox.

Uses of fractals

Fractals are also more than just a cool phenomenon — they have real-world uses that make them worth learning about, mostly in things that require a large surface area packed into a small amount of material. Antenna design is a great example: a fractal antenna can receive far more frequencies than a simple rod of the same size. Fractal math also shows up in image compression, search algorithms, and computer graphics used to generate natural-looking terrain.
